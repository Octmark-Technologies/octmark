Growth hacking is the pursuit of a repeatable, scalable tactic. Growth operations is the discipline of building the infrastructure that makes tactics work, stack, and compound.

They are not the same thing. Confusing them is expensive.

## The tactic trap

Growth hacking found its natural home in early-stage startups because early-stage startups need a thing that works right now. A viral loop. A referral mechanic. A distribution channel no one else has found yet.

The problem emerges at the growth stage, when you have something that works and need to make it scale. Tactics do not scale. Tactics are answers to specific questions at specific moments. When the question changes, the tactic stops working.

Growth-stage businesses that have built their entire operation on a stack of tactics spend enormous energy maintaining those tactics as they decay. They are always chasing the next one.

## What operations looks like instead

Growth operations asks a different question: what infrastructure, if it existed, would make any tactic we try more likely to work?

The answer is usually some combination of:

- A CRM that accurately reflects where every opportunity is and why
- Attribution that connects spend to revenue, not to clicks
- A customer health model that predicts churn before it happens
- A reporting layer that closes the loop between marketing and revenue

None of these are tactics. They are the conditions under which tactics become reliable.

## The compounding difference

Here is the practical distinction: a good tactic produces a spike. Good operations produces a floor.

When a growth-stage business builds operational infrastructure, it does not just improve the current metric, it raises the floor for every metric. Every future campaign runs on better data. Every future hire works with better systems. Every future decision is made with better information.

That is compounding. Tactics do not compound. Infrastructure does.

## The question to ask

Before the next growth initiative, ask one question: when this tactic stops working, and it will stop working, what will we have built that still works?

If the answer is nothing, you are running a tactic. If the answer is data, systems, or process, you are building operations.

Both have a place. Only one of them is still working in two years.
